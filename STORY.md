# THE BLOCK — Story

The narrative spine connecting all 10 stages. Reasons for the fights, beats per stage, and what each character is actually fighting *for*.

---

## Setup (pre-Stage 1)

**The Block** is a six-square-block neighborhood in a real American city — old brick walk-ups, a corner store, a hardware store, a community boxing gym, and the people who've lived there for two or three generations.

For ten years, **Victor Kane** — a real-estate developer with a suit, a smile, and an entire law firm — has been buying out the surrounding neighborhoods one block at a time. He calls it "revitalization." He shows up at city council meetings with charts. He never raises his voice.

Six months ago, Kane bought the lots under the Block. Lease-holders got a 30-day notice to vacate.

A week ago, Atlas's hardware store was bought out from under his lease. Yesterday, the gym — Marcus's gym, where Rio learned to fight — was padlocked, with a "FOR SALE — KANE PROPERTIES" sign nailed to the door.

Last night, Kane sent the first crew to "encourage" the holdouts.

This morning, the holdouts are fighting back.

## Why each character is here

### Rio
Marcus Stillman ran the gym on Lawrence and 5th for thirty-eight years. He found Rio when she was nineteen, sparring strangers for cash near the corner store. He took her in. He died last year — heart, no warning. The gym was the last place she had that felt like family.

When she sees the padlock, she doesn't hesitate. **Rio fights because Marcus's name is on that door.**

### Duke
Duke's apartment is on Garrison, three blocks from the gym. Rent's been stable for nine years because his landlord is an old man who refuses to sell. Last week the old man's daughter accepted Kane's offer; the old man died three days later, "natural causes." Duke knows what it looked like.

Duke isn't on a crusade. He just doesn't have anywhere else to go, and he's got nothing left to lose. **Duke fights because the alternative is leaving, and leaving means giving up the last fixed point in his life.**

### Atlas
Atlas has run Foster Hardware for twenty-three years. His wife is dead. His kids are in college. He's been holding the Block together legally and financially for two decades — paying lawyers, attending zoning meetings, refusing every offer. Every other neighbor has cracked at some point. He hasn't.

This week his lawyer told him there's nothing left to file. The eviction is enforceable in 14 days.

Atlas is the one who won't let Rio and Duke fight alone. **Atlas fights because if a 47-year-old hardware-store owner won't stand on his block, no one else will.**

## Stage-by-stage beats

Each stage is a different neighborhood Kane already owns or has just claimed. Geography moves outward from the Block, toward Kane's tower downtown.

### Stage 1 — THE BLOCK
*Wave 1*: Two **Runners** loitering outside the locked gym. They tell the protagonist "the gym's done." The protagonist asks if Kane sent them. They laugh.

*Wave 2*: Backup arrives — more Runners and a **Slice** (knife). The fight spills into the street. A taxi blasts by — Kane doesn't pay these guys to dodge traffic for him.

*Wave 3*: A **Chains** (heavy enforcer) shows up to finish what the Runners started.

*Stage clear*: The Block is quiet for the first time in a week.

### Stage 2 — THE TUNNELS
The protagonist follows the Runners' retreat into the subway. The next gang controls the platforms and tunnels. **Slices** patrol the corridors; the train rumbles through every minute and a half. *Bosses: none — this is a transit stage.*

### Stage 3 — RIVER ROW
Old industrial river district. Shipping containers, water at the edge. Kane uses this strip to move "uncooperative tenants." This is where **BARON** — Kane's old-school enforcer — runs his shakedown crew of **Tanks** and **Lamplights**.

**Boss: BARON.** He's the closest thing Kane has to a public face on the street. Brass knuckles. Ex-cop. Talks like he's negotiating.

*Stage clear narration*: "The cops won't come here. Baron used to be one of them."

### Stage 4 — LANTERN ROW
A market street being dismantled. Lanterns hang on wires across the alleys; some have already been cut and fall during fights. **Shades** (masked, fast) and **Slices** ambush from corners.

### Stage 5 — THE CAGE
Kane's people are using a defunct underground fight club as a holding pen. The walls have spike strips that extend on a timer — leftover from the cage matches. **Dojo** fighters here drilled in martial-arts forms; the **Rig** is a former heavyweight who works for Kane now.

### Stage 6 — UPTOWN
Glass-and-steel financial district. **RAZOR** runs intimidation in the office towers — knife-quick, polite, never raises her voice. Glass shards rain from the buildings she's been "renovating."

**Boss: RAZOR.** Kane's specialist for white-collar holdouts. The kind of person you only meet right before you sell.

### Stage 7 — THE WORKS
Foundry / steel mill that Kane's company is converting to lofts. Molten metal still flows in the floor channels because demolition starts next week. **Tanks** and **Rigs** patrol — these are the workers Kane's been keeping on payroll for the dirty jobs.

### Stage 8 — THE FREEWAY
Overpass at night. Kane's crews are cleaning out an encampment under the freeway. Cars blast across the road as you fight. **Chains** and **Slices** run this stretch.

### Stage 9 — THE TOWER
Rooftop of Kane Properties. Wind tears across the deck. **VOLT** — Kane's last lieutenant — has cybernetic implants Kane financed; he's the proof that Kane will use any tool to keep what he's bought. **Rigs** and **Dojos** form his guard.

**Boss: VOLT.** Heavy-handed cyber striker. Loyal because Kane gave him the legs back.

*Boss intro*: "Kane built me. So I owe him a building."

### Stage 10 — THE TOP
Penthouse. Floor-to-ceiling windows. A view of the Block from twelve miles up.

**Victor Kane** is sitting at a desk. He doesn't get up. He offers the protagonist a check. The protagonist refuses.

This is a **QTE cinematic** — Kane never throws a punch. The fight is symbolic: every press a refusal, every miss a moment Kane almost talks the protagonist into walking away. **You can't beat Kane with violence. You beat him by refusing the deal.**

When the cinematic ends, Kane's empire doesn't fall. The Block is saved for the year. Kane will be back. The story isn't *winning* — it's *not losing this round*.

## Difficulty tiers and what they say about the story

The four difficulty settings aren't just stat scalars — they reframe the run.

- **STORY** (player damage 0.5x, enemy HP 0.75x, enemies less aggressive). For players who want the *narrative*, not the challenge. Skip the boss-rush feeling and finish the arc.
- **NORMAL** (1.0x everything). The intended experience. Beat each boss honestly.
- **HARD** (player damage 1.5x, enemy HP 1.2x, enemies more aggressive). Kane's people are organized; you came underprepared.
- **BRUTAL** (player damage 2.0x, enemy HP 1.4x, enemies fastest). BLACKWELL appears as an extra encounter on stages 7 and 8. Kane has no patience left.

## Tone reminders for writers / designers

- **No revenge fantasy.** The protagonist isn't here to *win*. They're here because the alternative is *losing the place that made them*.
- **Kane never raises his voice.** Every interaction with his people should feel polite, then violent. Politeness is the menace.
- **The Block is the protagonist.** Cuts and scrapes on every fighter, but the building shadows are warm, the windows light up, the drizzle keeps falling. The neighborhood looks alive.
- **No glamour around violence.** Hits hurt. Hitstop and screen shake make every connection feel like work, not a flourish.
- **The ending is bittersweet.** Kane doesn't fall. The Block keeps standing for now. That's the win.
